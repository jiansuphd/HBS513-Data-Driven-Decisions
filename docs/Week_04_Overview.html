Why Design Matters - Improving Causal Inference Through Better Comparisons

Overview

In Week 3, we saw why cross-sectional regression often falls short as a tool for causal analysis: units differ in meaningful ways, reverse causation is hard to rule out, and unobserved confounders remain. Statistical controls can help, but—when not every confounder is known or can be measured, and when we can be certain of causal direction between X and Y—generally fall short.

This week, we pivot to solutions by introducing the logic of design-based inference. Rather than adjusting for confounders after the fact, we aim to structure “like to like” comparisons that are more credible from the outset—giving us stronger leverage for understanding cause and effect.

We focus on two core principles:

  • Compare like to like. When units are similar in relevant ways, differences in outcomes are easier to interpret. Researchers often exploit administrative thresholds, natural boundaries, or institutional rules to isolate more credible comparisons.
  • Use time as a tool. Observing how the same unit changes over time can sharpen inference and reduce bias—especially when good cross-sectional comparisons are hard to find.

These ideas form the foundation of quasi-experimental thinking. We’ll dig into specific designs next week, but this module builds the underlying intuition: better comparisons yield better causal claims.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, you will be able to:

  • Explain why causal inference depends on credible comparisons.
  • Describe how design-based inference improves on post hoc statistical control.
  • Identify strategies for comparing like to like.
  • Explain how time-based strategies help isolate causal effects.
  • Evaluate design logic in real-world public policy research.
