Difference-in-Differences

Overview

Week 12 introduces Difference-in-Differences (DiD), one of the most widely used designs for evaluating policy interventions in applied public policy and administration. DiD is designed for situations where some units receive a policy or program and others do not, and where outcomes for everyone are changing over time. The key idea is simple: DiD compares how treated units change before and after an intervention and subtracts out the change observed in untreated units. This isolates the portion of the treated group’s change that cannot be explained by broader forces affecting everyone. In this module, we’ll develop intuition for how DiD generates a credible counterfactual, examine the core assumption of parallel trends, and estimate a two-way fixed effects DiD model in Stata.

Learning Objectives

At the end of this week, you will:

  • Explain how Difference-in-Differences identifies causal effects by comparing changes in treated units to changes in untreated units over time.
  • Describe the intuition behind the DiD counterfactual and why untreated units serve as the benchmark for the treated group’s “no-policy world.”
  • Interpret DiD regression output—including the key treatment × post interaction term—using both graphical and algebraic reasoning.
  • Evaluate whether the parallel trends assumption is plausible in a given dataset and understand its importance for causal inference.
  • Implement a DiD model in Stata using two-way fixed effects and interpret results in an applied policy context.
