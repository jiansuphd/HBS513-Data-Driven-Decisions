Moving Beyond 512 — Cross-Sectional Data and the Problem of Causal Inference

Overview

In Week 3, we step back to critically examine the foundation of our past work: observational, cross-sectional data. While this approach allows us to describe patterns and associations, it does not support causal claims. Through real-world examples and academic studies, we explore how reverse causation and omitted variable bias challenge our interpretations and can mislead policy or administrative decisions.

This module builds on your knowledge of regression from HBS512 and explains why regression alone—especially in cross-sectional settings—cannot establish causality. The goal is to help you recognize the limitations of cross-sectional analysis and prepare you to adopt more rigorous strategies for credible, evidence-based research.

Learning Objectives

At the end of this week, you will be able to:

  • Explain what cross-sectional data are and how they limit causal inference.
  • Interpret the results of a simple (bivariate) OLS regression within a cross-sectional framework.
  • Identify and explain two major threats to causal inference in observational data: reverse causation and omitted variable bias.
  • Evaluate why regression alone—even with control variables—cannot eliminate bias or establish causality.
  • Distinguish between statistical association and causal explanation in empirical research.
  • Assess how cross-sectional limitations affect the validity of findings in published academic studies.
  • Identify how misinterpreting cross-sectional data can lead to ineffective or incorrect policy and management decisions.
  • Explain why stronger research designs are necessary for credible evidence-based policymaking.
